Instructor data dashboard

The large amount of data Coursera gathers from 10 mil learners leads to statistically significant insights. Coursera instructor dashboards are one of competitive advantages of the platform; they provide key statistics, including courser ratings, learner feedbacks, and demographics to our partners. With these data instructors measure the pulse of the course, pinpoint statistic problem spots, and provosts and instructional designers make informed decisions for future courses.

Starting with Course Dashboard design in 2014, I worked on all three Coursera user-facing dashboard products and currently working on the fourth. As the designer in charge of the projects, I worked on the full stages of process from needfinding that led to product and metrics desicions (in collaboration with Product Managers), to wireframes, visual design, and supporting engineers through implementation.

Process


Soon after the project started it became clear that we had surplus of data on our hands, but little understanding of what stories we should tell with it.

So I set out to understand instructors' mindsets. What are the motivations behind running a Coursera course? What metrics would they find rewarding or meaningful? What are the questions they would seek answers for on our dashboards? And last but not least, what would make the dashboards actionable?


Storybard helped us form the hypothesis of the emotional and functional role dashboards play.

Research session with professors provided validation to the hypothesis and insights.

Insights led to selection of metrics and its definition.

The research led to design directives.
  1. 1. In order to make the metrics meaningful, provide context and comparison data within and outside a course.
  2. 2. Balance insights and advice with feel good factor to keep users motivated.
  3. 3. The level of data-saviness and needs around data is widely different from one partner to another. Layer the information in a way that it can serve different level of details.

With these directives in hand, I developed sets of sketches and wireframes that went through several iterations based on internal and external feedback.


Concept to product


So far I have developed three Coursera dashboards - Partner, Specialization, and Course. Each dashboard serves different purposes depending on who the main users are and what the dashboards are used for.

Partner dashboard is designed to provide a partner with a top-level overview of the key metrics across all their courses on Coursera. It's the 'Big numbers' dashboard where metrics are used to motivate our partners and re-enforce parnter-Coursera alignment. January 2016. All metrics are placeholders.



The metric hierarchy on Specializaionts dashboard reflect the most salient questions course teams ask when they launch and run Specializations - how much revenue is it generating? What is the retention between courses? September 2015. All metrics are placeholders.



Course dashboard consists of four sub menus - Reach, Content, Ratings, and Stories. The dashboard is intended to deliver detailed insights on the health of course content. First development October 2014 then redesigned in November 2015. All metrics are placeholders.

Since it's first introduction in 2014, the course dashboard continuously evolved. As we introduced Learner Ratings & Feedback in 2015, we replaced the behaviourial metrics on Content dashboard with more obective learner problem reports and feedback metrics. The flow allows users to easily monitor the flags and take action on the problem, thus making the dashboard truly actionable. The close integration of metric dashboard with content editor is currently pending a US patent.


Afteworking on multiple dashboard projects, I initiated to modularize dashboard elements for efficient design and engineering process.


The Result


The actionable data had direct impact to user behavior and content quality. Partners are using the Specialization dashboard to monitor and share the performance of their content and to calculate ROI. Course dashboard to monitor more direct learner responses to a course and iterate on the content. For example, a course team improved a course rating by two points (2.8 to 4.8) by addressing the issues that were brought up by learners through the feedback UI.
